3. Demographic and Anthropometric data

This section documents how we obtain anthropometric data on the adult population in Singapore.

3.1 Obtaining Lower Limb Dimensions

To determine the frame dimensions, key anthropometric parameters of Singaporean adult males and females were referenced from Lee et al. (2019) [9]. Lower-limb length was estimated using segments 18 and 22, while foot dimensions were derived from segments 35 and 36 all of which are shown in the illustration below.

We then tabulated the combined anthropometric data corresponding to measurements in centimetres for both male and female participants containing the Combined Mean with ± 3 Standard Deviation, accommodating about 99.7% of Singapore’s adult population under a normal distribution (sample size ≈ 100).

In addition, we also obtained date from a nationally representative data from the Centre for Ageing Research and Education (CARES) at Duke-NUS. These data was derived from the PHASE study of Singaporeans aged 60 and above with cerebrovascular disease (e.g. Stroke), which provided popliteal height measurements corresponding to segment number 18 in the table above. Combining these with Lee et al. (2019) extends the range to better represent the local population including the elderly [9].

3.3 Weight

Using data from Roystonn (2021), the mean weight of adult male Singaporeans is 72.7 ± 14.4 kg [12]. In consideration of safety, the device is designed to withstand up to 150 kg, or roughly twice the mean weight. Additionally, mean weights and standard deviations for elderly stroke clients (≥65 years) were obtained from PHASE to further validate load requirements.

To derive segment weights from body weight, Figure 8 (Ramachandran et al, 2016) presents the percentages as part of the whole body weight of the thigh, calf, and foot [13]. By taking the average of the body segment percentages from each investigator, we obtained 10.70%, 4.59%, and 1.68% with a combined percentage of approximately 16.97% for the lower limb.

Applying this percentage to a 150 kg individual yields an estimated lower limb weight of 25.50 kg, which was used to guide the subsequent mechanical load requirements and the design phase.
