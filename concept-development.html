4.4 Mechanical Design

4.4.1 Design Inspiration

The device uses a ball screw conveyor driven by a motor to provide linear motion along the aluminum profile. The base plate moves linearly as the screw rotates, transferring motion to Rod 2, which connects to the footrest through mechanical links and rod ends. As the base plate slides, the linkage system converts the linear motion into controlled angular movement of the footrest, simulating lower-limb flexion and extension.

Several pain points were identified in this design. Firstly, many components are difficult to fabricate, increasing production complexity and cost. Additionally, the design relies on custom rod assemblies rather than standard OEM parts, limiting interchangeability and ease of maintenance. The overall structure is also not sufficiently sturdy, as the main frame supporting the legs consists of only a few rods, compromising stability and durability. Lastly, the design is heavily based on a Continuous Passive Motion (CPM) concept, which restricts functionality to passive movement and does not accommodate active or resistive rehabilitation modes.

4.5 Sensing Design Concepts

This section explains three different design concepts for the sensing subsystem in terms of their working principle and evaluation through a comparison matrix. All raw data will be transmitted to a processor, where signal noise will be filtered out for further analysis.

4.5.1 FIMU (Force + Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU)) Design Concept

The IMU subsystem combines an accelerometer, gyroscope, and optionally a magnetometer to measure motion and angular velocity across three axes, enabling analysis of hip, knee, and ankle joint kinematics. While gait analysis systems often use up to nine sensors, this concept employs a simpler four-IMU setup (lower back, thigh, shank, and foot), sufficient for single-limb rehabilitation./p>

The force sensor setup targets key pressure points on the foot—hallux, metatarsals, midfoot, and heels—mirroring the layout of commercial systems like the MatScan Pressure Mat (Guevara et al., 2024). For medial-lateral force sensing, additional sensors are placed near the medial and lateral malleoli to measure forces during hip adduction and abduction, following hip dynamometry assessment protocols (Thorborg et al., 2010).

4.5.2 Hall-Force-Flex (HFF) Design Concept

The HFF subsystem combines Hall-effect sensors, force sensors, and flex sensors to capture biomechanical data during lower-limb rehabilitation exercises for stroke clients. These sensors are positioned at key regions of the lower limb to track motion and loading across multiple planes of movement. The diagram illustrates possible placement zones, serving as a guide rather than an exact indication of the number and precise location of sensors required in each region, both of which are subject to the mechanical design of Kinetix. Force sensor placement in the HFF is similar to that of the FIMU design concept.

4.5.3 Computer Vision Design Concept

A computer vision sensor uses an RGB camera and pose-estimation algorithms to track 3D joint positions (e.g., hip, knee, ankle) and compute joint angles from relative body segment orientations. Its key advantages are practicality, cost-efficiency, and non-intrusiveness, as it requires no physical sensors and can operate with a simple camera in real-world settings. However, its accuracy declines for non-sagittal or obstructed movements and is sensitive to camera placement and angle, limiting precision in complex motion analysis (Wang et al., 2021). In this method, markers are placed on key joints (torso, hip, knee, ankle, and foot tip), and the client’s movements are recorded in 2D. The video is then processed using Tracker Software to determine marker positions and calculate joint angles.

4.7 Electrical Design

With mechanical subsystems finalised, the electrical design phase can begin. It involves evaluating suitable motors for active and passive modes, and selecting an appropriate microcontroller unit (MCU) for data processing and control. Before motor selection, the maximum torque that the screw bar can resist before backdriving must be determined to guide motor choice (Collins, n.d.).

Using a 150 kg load and a 10 mm screw lead, the calculated backdriving torque is 2.15 Nm. Hence, the selected motors must have a holding torque ≥ 2.15 Nm to ensure safe and reliable operation.

4.7.1 Component Table

Figure 8 provides a brief description of the motor types that we have considered for our device. Starting with Brushed DC Motor, its simple design meant that it is inexpensive but suffers from a short lifespan due to mechanical friction and noise. Hence, they are usually used in small toys and robotics. In contrast, BLDC offers greater efficiency and longer service life, thereby making them ideal for high-performance applications (Millett, n.d.; Seeed Studio, 2018). Meanwhile, Servo Motors have two sub-categories, which are positional and continuous rotation servos. The former has limited range of motion, but compensates it with excellent precision control due to its self-locking mechanism. However, the latter allows for unrestricted rotation, but lacks the capability of its counterpart to maintain a static position. Thus, positional servos are often used for camera stabilisation, whereas continuous rotation servos are mainly seen in robotic joints for smooth movement (Maeschke, 2023). Lastly, Stepper Motors have excellent torque at static positions and low speeds which is a result of its multiple poles design. Therefore, they are ideal for applications in holding heavy loads and maintaining a position such as a camera slider. Unfortunately, they are inefficient as they continuously draw a large current to maintain a position, often requiring cooling to prevent overheating (Seeed Studio, 2018; Tang, 2021).

4.7.2 Motor Evaluation

The stepper motor was selected for its high torque at low speeds and ability to maintain stable fixed positions, ideal for active mode, where users must overcome a preset force threshold to move the motor without backdriving. Its simple open-loop control avoids issues like jitter found in closed-loop systems under static loads. The chosen motor, a Nema 23 Bipolar Stepper Motor, provides a holding torque of 3.0 Nm, exceeding the 2.15 Nm backdriving torque requirement.

4.7.3 Microcontroller Unit (MCU) Evaluation

The Raspberry Pi Pico W2 was chosen as the device’s microcontroller unit (MCU) for its strong price-to-performance ratio, compact size, and built-in Wi-Fi and Bluetooth. Despite costing only S$9.62, it offers processing speed and memory comparable to higher-end options like the Teensy 4.1 (S$50–58). Its small footprint minimizes device size and leaves room for other components, while integrated wireless connectivity reduces costs and wiring complexity.
