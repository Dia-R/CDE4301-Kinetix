1. Introduction

1.1 Abstract

In Singapore, stroke is the fourth leading cause of death and the seventh leading cause of adult disability (World Stroke Organisation, 2017). This project involves the development of the robotic rehabilitation device, Kinetix, aimed at supporting motor recovery in stroke patients, with a focus on supine and seated rehabilitation. Kinetix combines mechanical actuation with sensor feedback to assist joint movement, monitor performance, and adapt to individual patient needs. It also features an app-based interface for user interaction and progress tracking. Control strategies and feedback mechanisms are explored to encourage active engagement and promote recovery.

1.2 Background

1.2.1 Types of Stroke

Strokes are largely either ischaemic or hemorrhagic in nature. Ischemic stroke occurs when a blood vessel is blocked, depriving a region of the brain of blood flow. Hemorrhagic stroke, on the other hand, involves bleeding in the brain due to a ruptured blood vessel. (Stroke Services Improvement, 2017). This section serves to explore the clinical features of a stroke contextualised to Singapore with an emphasis on the lower limb.

1.2.2 Clinical Features of Stroke

Clinical features of stroke include strength deficits, sensory-motor impairment, spasticity, incoordination, and postural dysfunction (Darak, 2020). Impaired motor control and weakness of extremities after stroke not only reduce muscular force generation but also affect the inter-limb coordination of functional movements, or synergies (Darak, 2020). The table below summarises the clinical features of a stroke with a focus on lower limb function, cognitive impairment, and neuropsychiatric symptoms.

Hemiplegia of the lower limb manifests as a severe reduction or loss of voluntary movement and muscle strength on the affected side. Gait disturbances are common, including difficulties with balance, foot clearance, and controlling lower-limb joints.

1.2.3 Stroke Recovery and Assessment

Stroke recovery includes broad improvement over multiple biological and neurological aspects of the body to enhance motor performance and establish activity-based behavioural outcomes (Sithamparapillai, 2017). This recovery is commonly assessed using standardised tools such as the Fugl-Meyer Assessment for lower extremity function, the Brunnstrom Stages of Motor Recovery, the Modified Ashworth Scale for spasticity (Harb, 2025), and the Motor Assessment Scale (MAS), with some assessments being more suitable for patient communication than others.

In addition, there are other assessment modes, such as the Barthel Index (BI), which measures functional independence and EQ-5D-3L, which measures overall health-related quality of life (HRQoL).

2. Literature Review on Existing Devices

This literature review aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the existing approaches to lower extremity rehabilitation in stroke patients, including both traditional and device-assisted rehabilitation, for the purpose of identifying user needs and determining the design concept of a novel rehabilitation device.

2.1 Clinical Background on Stroke Rehabilitation of Lower Extremities

Fatigue is a common complaint post-stroke, and is evident even in those individuals who have made an otherwise complete recovery. → so the robot device can reduce mental load and encourage recovery (Sithamparapillai, 2022)[a]. Lower extremity rehabilitation, especially for gait recovery, can require significant time and physical effort on the part of physiotherapists. Not only is this an expensive and subsequently unrealistic approach for rehab, but it is also extremely labour-intensive for the therapists to provide this care. Physiotherapists also report high rates of burnout due to long hours and proximity to patients, so the device might be a good idea (Nair, 2023). Another study examining challenges faced by physiotherapists during lower limb prosthesis rehabilitation reported that 81.5% of physiotherapists encountered "high physical demand" as a major challenge (Chi, 2022).

2.2 Existing Rehabilitation Methods

A range of rehabilitation techniques has been developed to support motor recovery following stroke, particularly for the lower extremities. These consist of both robotics-assisted and traditional forms of exercise.

One such approach is Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation (PNF), which is widely applied in clinical settings to improve balance and mobility in stroke patients (Li, 2022). Most studies agree that PNF is effective during the acute and subacute phases of recovery, where it supports neuromuscular re-education and promotes motor function. It is a hands-on manual technique applied by trained physiotherapists, incorporating movement across multiple planes to simulate functional tasks and help patients achieve specific recovery goals (Nguyen, 2022).

Robotic rehabilitation technologies such as powered overground exoskeletons, body weight-supported treadmill training (BWSTT) with driven gait orthoses (DGOs), and end-effector devices support gait restoration by assisting patient movement and partially offloading body weight. While exoskeletons enable level-ground walking, they often require sufficient upper limb strength to operate assistive devices. BWSTT and DGOs guide joint movements with robotic support and may still need manual therapist assistance in early stages, whereas end-effector devices use footplates on a pre-set gait path and have shown promising results when combined with conventional physiotherapy, especially for subacute stroke patients.  However, seated and supine centred devices occupy a niche in patients with more severe symptoms. As most existing technologies target patients from Brunnstrom Stage IV onward, this review highlights a significant gap in rehabilitation solutions for earlier stages, where patients may still be confined to supine or seated positions.

2.5 Gaps in current solutions

While some studies, such as Wang et al. (2016), explored the immediate effects of PNF on muscle tone and stiffness, their findings were limited by small sample size and lack of functional outcome measures. This highlights a broader issue in existing interventions that, while manual therapies like PNF show potential, they rely heavily on therapist skill, lack standardisation, and often fail to track long-term functional recovery. Moreover, there is a significant disparity between engineers who create devices for rehabilitation, and the underlying neuroscience related to motor deficits and rehabilitation after stroke (Hobbs, 2020). Robotic systems can take precise measurements of kinematic and dynamic values, which are far more reliable than those achieved with human error, and have the potential to be very useful for assessment purposes. (This being said, there remains a need to develop standardised procedures and protocols for this data to be useful. → Will move to future works)[b][c]. It also seems that the majority of such devices are for overground gait rather than supine and seated exercises.

2.5 Conclusion

The reviewed literature supports the integration of robotics into lower limb rehabilitation for stroke patients, both as a means of addressing workforce challenges and enhancing clinical outcomes. Traditional physiotherapy, while effective, is labour-intensive, dependent on therapist availability, and often difficult to sustain at the intensity required to drive neuroplasticity. Studies have highlighted the physical burden on physiotherapists and the variability in manual therapy delivery, particularly during prolonged or repetitive sessions. Robotic rehabilitation devices offer a promising alternative by delivering consistent, repeatable movement patterns and incorporating sensor-based feedback for objective monitoring of progress.

As existing systems primarily focus on gait restoration and ambulation, with limited attention given to patients in earlier Brunnstrom stages who may require rehabilitation in supine or seated positions, our proposed robotic device addresses these unmet needs by supporting early to medium stage rehabilitation. Our device aims to reduce the therapist’s physical workload and enhance patient engagement. The device will support both passive and active movements of the limb, including lateral movements. Furthermore, stroke patients often experience significant cognitive and emotional fatigue, which can hinder engagement in therapy. The inclusion of a user interface (UI) presents an opportunity to facilitate communication, provide feedback, and promote motivation during recovery. These developments strengthen the case for the continued evolution and clinical integration of robotic systems in stroke rehabilitation in lower limbs.

3. Design Methodology

For the scope of this project, the term “Patient” refers to individuals who have suffered a stroke and now require physiotherapy or rehabilitation to regain or improve the function of their lower limbs. Patients typically range in age from 40 to 80 years old, although younger stroke survivors may also require rehabilitation. However, recent studies and interviews with rehabilitation specialists have reported an increasing incidence of stroke victims from ages 40 years old and above (Kelly-Hayes, 2010; Lee et al., 2019; Interview C, 2025; Interview M, 2025). The average patient weight is expected to fall within 50 to 100 kilograms, with design considerations made to accommodate heavier patients to ensure safety and inclusivity (Kelly-Hayes, 2010; Lee et al., 2019). Patients may present varying levels of motor impairment, muscle strength, and cognitive ability, which will influence device usability requirements.

The term “Rehabilitation Specialist” refers collectively to physiotherapists, occupational therapists, kinesiologists, and other related healthcare professionals. Rehabilitation specialists may handle multiple patients throughout a day. Their primary role involves guiding, monitoring, and adjusting patient exercise routines while ensuring safety and adherence to rehabilitation goals.

Typical use cases for the device are expected to take place in hospice facilities, rehabilitation centres, and physiotherapy clinics. These environments will require the device to be durable, mobile, and adaptable to different patient needs and therapy settings. After consultations with the respective rehabilitation specialists, we intend to have the device be suited for the early to medium stages of stroke rehabilitation in a sub-acute setting (Interview B, 2025; Interview C, 2025; Interview M, 2025).

3.1.3 Weight[d][e]

The final step is to evaluate the approximate weight of our user, which is done by utilising a nationally representative sample of adult male Singaporeans with a mean age 43.9 years old from Roystonn’s (2021) [f]study and therefore, providing us a mean weight of 72.70 kg with a standard deviation of 14.40 kg.

For our device, it is necessary that it is able to handle loads far above the mean adult male Singaporean weight. Therefore, we decided that the device should handle twice the mean adult male Singaporean weight, which amounts to approximately 150 kg of load.

However, given that our device is oriented towards users who are both in a supine or sitting position, we still have to extrapolate the weight for their lower body. Ramachandran et al. (2016) compiled a table containing different research articles on body segment weights as a percentage of their total body weight, which can be seen below in Figure X.

Figure X: Compiled body segment weight percentage across different research studies

From Figure X, a mean value of 10.7%, 4.59% and 1.68% were obtained for thigh, calf and foot, respectively, across 6 different research studies. Thereby, when summing up the above values, it results in an approximate lower body segment weight percentage of 16.97%. When calculated against the mean adult male Singaporean weight of 150 kg, an approximate leg weight of 25.46 kg was obtained. With both the maximum leg and body weight accounted for, we could now proceed to the design phase.

4.4.1 Direct Current (DC) Motors (Brushed and Brushless)

DC motors are often classified into two main types which are brushed and brushless. These DC motors have two main components for its operation which are the rotor and stator (Brushless Vs Brushed DC Motors: When and Why to Choose One Over the Other | Article | MPS). The rotor is in charge of the movement of the motor which is affected by the magnetic field that is being induced at the stator. The working principle of DC motors is quite simple, but differs in operation between brushed and brushless DC motors. (DC Motor vs Stepper Motor vs Servo Motor - Which to choose?)

Figure X: Brushed DC Motor Diagram (Brushless Vs Brushed DC Motors: When and Why to Choose One Over the Other | Article | MPS)

In brushed DC motors, the stator is a permanent magnet while the rotor is an iron core that is composed of wound coils to create a magnetic field. In order to operate a brushed DC motor, an electrical current will travel through the commutator and into the wound coils. When a current travels through the windings, a magnetic field is induced that is equal in magnitude but in the opposite polarity to that of the stator. As a result, the rotor is then attracted to the stator due to their opposing polarity and thereby, causes the rotor to rotate towards the stator of opposing polarity. A commutator is then used to switch off the current temporarily and reverse the polarity of the induced magnetic field in the rotor windings by reversing the direction of the electric current. This process will then repeat and thus, causes the motor to rotate continuously.

Figure X: Brushless DC Motor Diagram (Brushless Vs Brushed DC Motors: When and Why to Choose One Over the Other | Article | MPS)

The brushless DC motor, meanwhile, operates in a different manner. In contrast to the brushless DC motor, the rotor is now a permanent magnet whereas the stator is now made up of wound coils which are called phases. Moreover, a mechanical commutator is not needed for the polarity to be reversed for continuous rotation but instead, the polarity is reversed through electronic commutation via switches. To operate the motor, an electric current will flow on one pair of phase windings which is the orange-coloured coils seen in the two-pole diagram in Figure X. A magnetic field is then induced in the stator which causes the rotor to be attracted towards the phase windings as long as they are of opposite polarity with each other. The switches within the brushless DC motor will now switch the current to another pair of phase windings, and thus, causing the motor to rotate continuously due to this repeated process.

Given the differences in their operation and construction, their applications are also suited for different demands (Brushless Vs Brushed DC Motors: When and Why to Choose One Over the Other | Article | MPS; DC Motor vs Stepper Motor vs Servo Motor - Which to choose?). With the brushed DC motor, they are often used for simple and low-cost applications that require handling light-moderate loads such as toys. However, their simple design means it has a poor service life and efficiency as it produces noise and heat due to the friction within the mechanical commutator. In contrast, the brushless DC motor has a significantly longer service life and efficiency due to the usage of electronic commutation. Another added benefit of electronic commutation is a larger torque range as it is not affected by the friction that occurs within the mechanical commutator in brushed DC motors and thus, makes them highly recommended for robotic applications.

4.4.2 Servo Motors

Servo motors operate similarly to a brushless DC motor, with some additional features integrated within its system. It contains a gearbox and an encoder that allows servo motors to operate at a higher torque range while providing a smooth motion profile due to its closed-loop control feedback system (DC Motor vs Stepper Motor vs Servo Motor - Which to choose?; The Choice Between Servo Motors and Stepper Motors).

Figure X: Servo Motor Diagram (3-Phase Stator)

Servo motors have two main types which are positional and continuous rotation servos. With a positional rotation servo, their range of motion is typically limited to 180 degrees, but compensates for this with excellent position control as a result of self-locking mechanisms that are integrated into the motor. These are often utilised in robotic arms to allow for movement in a limited range of motion. In contrast, the continuous rotation servo allows unrestricted rotation of the motor which is controlled via speed control (Positional versus Continuous Servos). In exchange for its continuous rotation, it is unable to hold a position like its counterpart and therefore, not applicable to handling external loads at a fixed position.

4.4.3 Stepper Motors

Stepper motors work in the same principle as DC motors, with the only difference being that it moves in discrete ‘steps’ (DC Motor vs Stepper Motor vs Servo Motor - Which to choose?; The Choice Between Servo Motors and Stepper Motors). In contrast to other DC motors, it has a larger number of poles that are further divided into smaller teeth. These teeth enable the stepper motor to move in small angle increments, allowing for precise precision control as seen in Figure X.

Figure X: Stepper Motor Diagram (The Choice Between Servo Motors and Stepper Motors)

It has excellent torque performance at low speeds, enabling it to resist heavy external loads without moving the motor. Therefore, they are commonly used in applications where maintaining a stable position is necessary such as camera sliders. However, its main downside is that it is relatively inefficient as it constantly draws a large amount of current in order to maintain its motor position. Therefore, a cooling system might be required to prevent it from overheating.

4.4.6 Operation Description

Figure X: Operation Flowchart Diagram

In order to operate the device, it must be plugged into the wall socket and physically turned on via a switch on the device. Upon being turned on, it will enter a ‘Warm-Up Mode’ where the MCU will command a yellow LED to switch on while also instructing the motor driver to drive the motor such that the footrest will be placed at its starting position, where the user will have its leg fully-flexed and with their knee close to their chest.

The physiotherapist will then select on his tablet the type of exercise as well as whether to operate it in either passive or active mode. Once the exercise and mode is confirmed, the information will be transferred to the MCU via Wi-Fi and instructs the device to move either horizontally or laterally, depending on the exercise, as well as light up the green LED to indicate that the exercise is running. For passive mode, there are no sensors that actively track the joint angle and force exerted by the user. Meanwhile, for active mode, the joint angle and force are constantly being monitored which is then sent onto a database for model processing for future exercise recommendations.

In the case of emergencies, there are two safety overrides within the device. The first safety override is manual where either the user or the physiotherapist can press the button to immediately stop the device. Once it is pressed again, it will then return back to its starting position. Meanwhile, the second safety override is located within the tablet where the physiotherapist can press the button to stop the device and another button press results in the footrest returning back to its starting position.

4.5 Sensing Design Concepts

This section explains three different design concepts for the sensing subsystem in terms of their working principle and evaluation through a comparison matrix. All raw data will be transmitted to a processor, where signal noise will be filtered out for further analysis.

4.5.1 FIMU (Force + Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU)) Design Concept

The IMU subsystem consists of an accelerometer, a gyroscope, and in some cases a magnetometer, enabling the measurement of motion across three axes and angular velocity to capture joint kinematics, specifically knee flexion/extension, ankle plantarflexion/dorsiflexion, and hip flexion/extension. A reference IMU system for gait analysis employs seven motion sensors and an additional two integrated into insoles containing force sensors. Since Kinetix focuses on single-limb rehabilitation rather than full gait symmetry analysis, a reduced configuration of four IMUs in the lower back, thigh, shank, and foot is sufficient to capture the relevant movement, as shown in the diagram below, noting that the choice of a left lower limb is for illustration purposes only.

IMU is widely used in Biomechanics analysis because of its ability to capture angular velocities and Joint angles. It is also lightweight, cost-effective and strapable (Rahman et al., 2023), which increases its accessibility. The IMU setup does not include a camera, unlike traditional biomechanical analysis, such as measuring gait kinematics (Manupibul et al., 2023), which provides for the ease of usage.

Despite these benefits, IMUs are prone to noise and magnetic field disturbance (Seel et al., 2020). The additive nature of the errors in IMU over time, relating to sensor noises, bias and magnetic field interaction, can lead to drift. Nonetheless, drift in IMUs can be corrected using the Allan Variance method (El-Shemy et al., 2008).

The force sensor component consists of sensor placement on the prominent parts which experience greater pressure than the less prominent ones (Guevara et al., 2024). This can be surmised to be the hallux, medial and lateral metatarsal, lateral midfoot, and medial and lateral heel. This setup also mimics the coverage of commercial plantar pressure measurement devices such as the MatScan Pressure Mat, also used for neuropathic feet.

Medial-lateral force sensing is conducted by placing a force sensor along the medial malleolus and lateral malleolus of the foot for hip adduction and hip abduction respectively, referencing hip dynamometry strength assessment tests (Thorborg et al., 2010).

Hall-effect, force, and flex sensors together provide a low-cost, lightweight, and relatively non-invasive way to capture joint kinematics and kinetics in real time. They are small and adaptable for integration into wearable devices, making them suitable for continuous monitoring during rehabilitation exercises (Patel et al., 2012; Yang & Hsu, 2010). Their combined use allows both direct measurements (e.g., force) and indirect estimations (e.g., ROM, fatigue), covering a wide range of clinically relevant metrics.

These sensors are susceptible to noise, drift, and placement errors, which can reduce accuracy without careful calibration and signal processing (Chen et al., 2020). Flex and Hall sensors in particular only provide indirect estimates of joint angles, which may not capture complex 3D movements as precisely as IMUs or optical systems (Cutti et al., 2008). Additionally, durability under repeated mechanical stress and patient comfort can be limiting factors in long-term use.

4.5.3 Computer Vision Design Concept

A computer vision sensor uses a standard RGB camera to measure joint angles by recording video of the patient’s movement and applying pose-estimation algorithms to identify and reconstruct the 3D positions of key body joints such as the hip, knee and ankle joint. From these reconstructed positions, the relative orientations of body segments are calculated to derive joint angles.

Computer Vision's major strengths lie in its practicality and cost-efficiency. Since it requires no physical sensors or markers placed on the subject, it is far less intrusive. It can be easily deployed in real-world environments using only a simple camera. The process is also fully automated, efficiently analysing movement without labour-intensive manual processing (Wang et al., 2021).

However, the limitations are tied to its technological constraints. Its precision diminishes when assessing finer, non-sagittal plane movements, including motions where a limb moves away from the body or rotates inward. Accuracy can also be compromised if the camera's view of a joint is obstructed by another body part or object, and the system's performance is sensitive to the camera's specific position and angle (Wang et al., 2021).

Markers are placed on the mid-torso, hip, knee and ankle joints, and the tip of the foot. The patient will then be recorded by a camera doing the various lower limb exercises in a 2D plane. The recorded video will then be uploaded into the Tracker Software to locate the position of markers throughout the exercises, leading to the derivation of hip, knee and ankle joint angles through calculations.




The usage of Tracker software is straightforward and requires only brightly coloured markers, such as adhesive tapes, and a standard phone camera. This simple setup makes the system highly cost-effective and accessible.

However, due to the simplicity of the design, there are several shortcomings. Placement of the phone camera can lead to deviation of the calculated joint angles, especially when the planes of movement are not aligned. The additional step of transferring the captured video into the Tracker software and processing it manually compromises the user-friendliness of the concept.

4.6 Data Processing and Analysis

In physical therapy, machine learning has been proposed as a valuable tool for analyzing patient movements using data from wearable devices (Reis et al., 2024). Beyond motion analysis, machine learning can be employed to predict outcomes, design individualized care plans, monitor adherence and performance of exercises at home, and even assess a patient’s emotional state (Reis et al., 2024). Collectively, these capabilities suggest that machine learning has significant potential to enhance the quality and effectiveness of physical rehabilitation of stroke patients.
