2. Literature Review on Stroke Rehabilitation Background and Methods

This section aims to provide a comprehensive overview of challenges, existing approaches to lower extremity rehabilitation in stroke patients, including both traditional and device-assisted rehabilitation, to identify a value proposition, user needs, and determine the design concept of a novel rehabilitation device.

2.1 Challenges in Stroke Rehabilitation of Lower Extremities

Lower-limb rehabilitation places substantial physical and time demands on physiotherapists. This burden contributes to high levels of burnout, with physiotherapists reporting stress from long working hours and the physically intensive nature of hands-on therapy (Nair, 2023). 81.5% of physiotherapists identified “high physical demand” as a major challenge in their practice (Chi, 2022). These challenges highlight the need for assistive rehabilitation technologies that can reduce therapist workload while sustaining patient engagement and recovery outcomes.

2.2 Stroke Rehabilitation Methods

Stroke rehabilitation typically integrates manual therapies and robotic-assisted systems across different stages of recovery.

Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation (PNF) is a widely used manual technique that:

  • supports neuromuscular re-education
  • is therapist-guided, mimicking real functional tasks
  • is particularly effective in the acute to sub-acute phases (Li, 2022; Nguyen, 2022)

Continuous Passive Motion (CPM) machines are also implemented early in recovery to:

  • provide repetitive movement that promotes neuroplasticity
  • enhance post-stroke outcomes (Ripin, 2018)

In contrast, robotic rehabilitation systems such as Body Weight Supported Treadmill Training (BWSTT) are typically introduced in the late subacute to chronic stages to:

  • Provide repetitive, task-specific movement training aimed at improving walking ability and functional independence.
